Water Kefir

Water kefir is a fermented drink made from water and sugar with high counts of diverse probiotic strains. It tastes lightly fizzy and sour with varying sweetness depending on how long the fermentation goes. Many people find water kefir helps with digestive issues like food intolerances or IBS, and it's suitable for vegans unlike milk kefir (though milk kefir is more diverse if you're not vegan.)

Making Water Kefir

You can scale this up or down as needed depending on how many grains you have. You can also play around with the amounts and fermentation time to find what works for you. Relatively more liquid will increase fermentation time, while more grains will ferment faster.

What You Need

  • Water kefir grains, 1 tablespoon
  • Dechlorinated tap water, 250ml-500ml (leave a jug of tap water out overnight)
  • White or raw sugar, 1 tablespoon per 250ml-500ml
  • A pinch of salt
  • A dried fig and a slice of lemon (optional but provides trace nutrients not found in refined sugar)
  • A jar for fermenting with some way to release pressure, like an airlock, swing-top, or cheesecloth
  • Pressure bottles to store kefir in the fridge, like swing tops or old soft drink bottles

Primary Ferment

  1. Mix the sugar, salt, and water until mostly dissolved
  2. Add the kefir grains and the water solution to the fermenting jar
  3. Add the dried fig and lemon slice
  4. Put the fermenting jar on the bench or in a cupboard, out of direct sunlight
  5. After 24-48 hours, strain to separate the liquid from the solids
  6. Put the liquid into the storage bottle in the fridge, this is the kefir you drink
  7. Start again at step 1 with the grains in the sieve, you can reuse the fig and lemon a time or two

Secondary Ferment

Add extra flavours to the kefir in the fridge in a "secondary fermentation." You can try fresh fruit or juice, cordial, crystallised ginger or other dried fruit, small amounts of spices or tea leaves, etc. The kefir will keep fermenting any residual sugars and any new sugars you add. You can leave it at room temperature to get the secondary fermentation going faster, or put it straight in the fridge to slow it down. A few days to a week in the fridge is often a good amount, the flavour and sweetness will change as it ferments. Have fun!

FAQ

What is a primary ferment?

This is just the initial fermentation you do by putting the kefir grains in a container with sugar water and letting it ferment for a day or two. It finishes once you strain the kefir grains out and start the process again.

Keep this step relatively simple and don't add extra ingredients. Too much sugar at this stage can cause the microbes to get out of balance. Things can get a bit slimy or overly yeasty, etc.

What is a secondary ferment?

Additional fermentation that happens after you've removed the kefir grains from the liquid, usually in the fridge with some extra fruit or flavouring in the bottle.

After your primary ferment is finished and you've strained the grains out, you're left with the kefir you consume. At this point, the kefir is inoculated with all the bacteria and yeast from the kefir grains and will keep fermenting any residual sugars (and a bunch of other reactions.) Putting it in the fridge slows this extra fermentation down so it doesn't run out of sugars or get overly acidic. It's really up to you, it'll happily eat new sugars of any kind or be just as happy without.

Keep in mind that you're only adding these to the kefir you put in the fridge to consume, NOT in the primary ferment with the kefir grains.

What kind of container should I use?

For the primary ferment you want a jar or container between 0.5L and 2L depending how much you want to make at once (you could go larger, it just gets unwieldy.) I find water kefir easy to drink so I make around 1.5L every 2 days. You could use food grade plastic or glass containers as long as they're designed to release pressure during fermentation. I use a large swing-top glass jar from Muji that naturally vents pressure before it gets explosive.

Again, you want something that can handle pressure build up as water kefir can get quite fizzy. Swing top glass bottles, old soft drink bottles, or anything designed for beer or wine should work fine.

What should my water kefir look, smell, and taste like?

Straight out of the primary ferment it should taste and smell lightly acidic, mildly sweet, a little lemony or funky but not overly strong. You can often see light carbonation at this point. You can get different smells and flavours depending on the kind of sugar you use, the temperature you're fermenting at, whether you put figs and/or lemon slices in, etc. but mine hasn't ever been particularly intense after the primary ferment. The colour varies from a cloudy white for white sugar to yellow or orange for raw and brown sugars.

The secondary ferment is likely to smell and taste like whatever you added to the kefir, plus a light acidity and some fizz. Again, it's not usually too strong tasting and it can be sweet or dry depending on how much sugar went into it thus far and how long it had to ferment that sugar away.

Should I cover my primary ferment with a lid or cheesecloth?

You can do either and you'll see varied beliefs online. I haven't noticed much of a difference either way so I just do what's easy. I believe oxygen flow (if you use cheesecloth for example) favours slightly different sets of microbes than a sealed lid but the kefir as a whole will be happy either way and you'll still be getting all the benefits. Do what's easy and works for you.

I personally use a swing-top jar that lets carbon dioxide escape but is otherwise sealed, so limited oxygen for the kefir.

What temperature should I ferment at?

If the temperature is comfortable for you then it's also good for your kefir. 15-25C is ideal but it'll work outside of that too. Cooler will slow fermentation while hotter will speed it up. If it's uncomfortably hot for you where you're kefir is (say, above 30C) then you probably want to move it somewhere cooler. Your grains won't die unless they get above 40C for extended periods of time.

Is water kefir alcoholic?

Very slightly but it's usually low enough to be classified as a non-alcoholic beverage in Australia. It's unlikely to get over 1% alcohol by volume, usually less. I believe kefir produces about a quarter of the alcohol of brewers yeast for the same sugar inputs so you can theoretically optimise for higher alcohol but it's hard and you might as well make beer or wine at that point.

If you're finding it smells more yeasty or alcoholic than normal, try giving your grains a rinse under cold tap water before you do your next batch. Sometimes the yeasts can become more dominant and a wash seems to help. Even so, the alcohol content will never be high.

Can I use honey/milk/sugar replacement

Nope.

Honey is way too rich (very fructose heavy) and has its own complex microbial and antimicrobial stuff going on so it's just not a good idea to mix the two.

Milk kefir and water kefir are distinct, you can't use water kefir grains to make milk kefir or visa-versa. You might be able to ferment coconut water or some plant milks with water kefir grains but make sure you've got backup grains in case it all goes horribly wrong and you have to discard your experiment.

Sugar replacements are a straight up nope. Water kefir feeds on the sugar and sugar replacements have no sugar so the kefir grains will starve. Some sugar replacements even kill microbes because they look close enough to sugar that the microbe eats it but then provide no energy so the microbe dies.

If you're worried about the sugar content, try leaving the kefir in the fridge for a week and only adding minimal extra sugar for the second ferment. Water kefir can be quite dry if you let the microbes convert all the sugar to acids and other metabolites.

I have too many kefir grains

Happy grains will multiply, especially in warmer weather. This isn't necessarily a problem but can speed up your primary fermentation. I find water kefir grains are quite forgiving and I can make large or small amounts with varied amounts of grains, but you might want to slow it down.

Here are some options to deal with having too many:

  • Put some to sleep. There's an FAQ section on taking a break, check that out. This doesn't get rid of them, but it does give you a backup if you want to experiment.
  • Give them to friends. They'll need a tablespoon or two to get started.
  • Blend them into a smoothie, they're apparently very good for you.
I'm going away / need a break

No worries, you can put your kefir grains to sleep for weeks to months at a time and they should be OK.

For up to a few days, just put the grains in a jar in the fridge. For weeks, cover the grains with a small amount of sugar water and put it in the fridge, keeping in mind that it can still build up pressure during that time so either use a swing-top jar or open the lid every now and then.

You can also freeze kefir grains for months and restart the later. I've never done so but you can search online and find some instructions.

They might be a little sluggish when you first pull them out of the fridge, especially if they've been in there a while, but they should perk back up if you start a regular rhythm again.

What are the kefir grains made of?

Their structure is provided by a polysaccharide called dextran, similar to cellulose, that one of the bacteria in the water kefir grains produces. Dextran clumps together to form the grains, and the grains provide a protective layer around the entire colony of microorganisms that live in there.

Dextran is perfectly safe to eat so there's no worries about eating the grains, accidentally or otherwise.

How clean does everything have to be?

I wash out my primary fermenting jar once every few weeks or if I notice lots of sediment. I was my storage bottles every time I finish the kefir in them, but you could keep refilling them for a while too. The kefir colony seems to murder any pathogens that get into it. I obviously use clean utensils and wash my strainer well each time, but the kefir grains seem quite robust as long as they're happy and healthy. Regular kitchen cleanliness seems to be enough.

Fermenting websites love to talk about everything being perfectly sterile for each batch and that seems important for commercial production, but it's way overkill for home fermentation.

Where can I get kefir grains?

If you have a friend who makes water kefir, ask them for some. The grains replicate on their own so they'll have too many at some point. You only need around 1 tablespoon to get started.

You can also buy some online, they'll survive express post. I bought mine from The Fermentary but there are plenty of others.

How does water kefir compare to probiotic tablets?

Milk kefir has more research behind it and seems to have higher diversity and more colony-forming units than water kefir, but water kefir still seems to have many of the same beneficial bacteria in relatively high amounts.

All kinds of kefir will have more active probiotics than probiotic tablets because dried probiotics aren't very active. The specific strains in the kefir will vary by local environment, climate, and the grains you started with, similar to sourdough starter.

From what I've read anecdotally, water kefir has similar success stories for stomach issues as milk kefir.
