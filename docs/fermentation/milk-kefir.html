Milk Kefir

Milk kefir is a fermented drink made from animal milk with high counts of diverse probiotic strains.

Making Milk Kefir

What You Need

  • milk kefir grains, 1-4 tablespoons
  • Animal milk, 500-1000ml
  • A jar for fermenting
  • A bottle to store kefir in the fridge

Method

  1. Put the kefir grains in the fermenting jar with the milk, covered loosely with the lid or cheesecloth so gas can escape
  2. Put the fermenting jar in a cupboard or other dark place for 24-48 hours
  3. Strain the contents of the fermenting jar with a course sieve to separate the grains and the liquid
  4. Put the liquid into the storage bottle in the fridge, this is the kefir you drink
  5. Start again at step 1 with the grains in the sieve

Optional

You can add extra flavours to the kefir in the fridge in a "secondary fermentation." Lemon peel is a great place to start as it helps cut some of the more funky kefir flavours, but you can try other things too. Spices, fresh fruit, dried fruit, vanilla essence, etc. The kefir will ferment any new sugars you add, slowly in the fridge or faster out of the fridge if you want that. Have fun!

FAQ

What is a primary ferment?

This is just the initial fermentation you do by putting the kefir grains in a container with milk and leaving it somewhere dark like a cupboard for a day or two. It finishes once you strain the kefir grains out and start the process again.

This should only be done with animal milk, don't add fruit or extra sugars to your primary ferment.

What is a secondary ferment?

Additional fermentation that happens after you've removed the kefir grains from your milk (now kefir,) usually in the fridge with some extra fruit or flavouring in the bottle.

After your primary ferment is finished and you've strained the grains out, you're left with the kefir you consume. At this point, the kefir is inoculated with all the bacteria and yeast from the kefir grains and will keep fermenting any residual sugars (and a bunch of other reactions.) Putting it in the fridge slows this extra fermentation down so it doesn't run out of sugars or get overly acidic too quickly. Some people like to add fresh or dried fruit at this point, or just flavour it a bit with some vanilla essence or some lemon peel. It's really up to you, it'll happily eat new sugars of any kind, or be just as happy without.

Keep in mind that you're only adding these to the kefir you put in the fridge to consume, NOT in the primary ferment with the kefir grains.

What kind of container should I use?

For the primary ferment, you want a jar or wide-mouth bottle around 0.5-1L depending how much you want to make at once. Food grade plastic is convenient because it won't explode from pressure or smash if it slips out of your hands. You can also use glass or stainless steel, just be aware of pressure build up during fermentation that can explode glass that's not meant to handle it (which is extremely messy.) Some metal jar lids can also react to the acids in the kefir and get rusty, so watch out for that and stop using those if it happens.

The secondary ferment is a bit easier because it's fermenting much slower in the fridge. I use glass milk bottles with metal lids that don't seem to react to the acids in the kefir. I haven't had any major pressure buildup yet and I open the bottles most days anyway to get some kefir. You want something easy to clean with a wide mouth for pouring thick kefir into or adding fruit, etc.

My kefir smells funky (or off)

Yep! It's probably fine, though.

It smells worst right as you're straining and bottling it. It's warm, sour, yeasty, and full of carbon dioxide. Mine smells like a cross between warm beer and yoghurt at this stage. It'll smell less funky once it's cold, and some lemon peel in the second ferment really helps cut the funk as well.

Another possibility: sunlight (or any kind of light) can make milk products taste weird, think cheese or butter left on the bench for a few hours, so make sure you're fermenting in a dark place or an opaque container.

Part of the difficulty is that we're not used to leaving milk out of the fridge so weird smells seem scary. I've never had any milk kefir go off as healthy grains will populate the milk very rapidly and prevent any other bacteria or moulds from getting started. Remember, fridges haven't been around very long! I've read that milk kefir lasts for weeks unrefrigerated, though I've never tried it myself.

Of course, there is always a chance that you legitimately have an off batch. This could happen because your grains are very weak or dead, or your milk was already off. You could try giving your grains a good wash in cold tap water and making another batch, or just throw the grains out and get some fresh ones. Milk kefir is very robust so this seems unlikely to me, but it's always a possibility.

My kefir has split/separated

That's fine, it's a little over-fermented but not off. Shake the kefir up to combine it again and strain it as usual.

Shaking will increase the pressure (like shaking a bottle of lemonade) so open the container in the sink to avoid explosions and be extra careful if you're using a glass container that isn't made to withstand pressure.

It might taste a bit stronger or more acidic than normal but there's nothing wrong with it. Primary fermentation speeds up in warmer temperatures so you can try keeping it in a cooler part of the house or reducing the fermentation time if you'd rather avoid this.

Should I cover my primary ferment with a lid or cheesecloth?

You can do either and you'll see varied beliefs online. I haven't noticed much of a difference either way so I just do what's easy.

I lightly place the lid over the top of my jar without screwing it down. That allows carbon dioxide to escape and keeps moulds and other bacteria out. The kefir grains are happy with or without oxygen and some people use cheesecloth instead to provide more air.

Is kefir alcoholic?

Very slightly but it's usually low enough to be classified as a non-alcoholic beverage in Australia. It's unlikely to get over 1% alcohol by volume, usually much less.

If you're finding it smells more like alcohol than normal, try giving your grains a rinse under cold tap water before you do your next batch. Sometimes the yeasts can become more dominant and a wash seems to help. Even so, the alcohol content will never be high.

Can I use plant milk instead of animal milk?

Maybe, but I would advise using animal milk until you're comfortable with the process and have plenty of spare grains to experiment with.

I have read of people successfully using various kinds of plant milk for short periods of time, but they're not as nutritious as animal milk so the kefir grains can get weaker over time. Your best bet is to alternate between animal and plant milk on a schedule that keeps them healthy.

I have also read of someone completely converting their milk kefir grains to live a vegan lifestyle for a year or more, but they were making their own plant milk with blended dates, seeds, nuts, etc. The bacteria in kefir do seem to eat all kinds of sugars so this does seem doable, but probably best done like this with whole foods to keep the bacteria healthy. This is not commonly done so I'd say keep some spare grains in the fridge in case it all goes wrong and you want to start over.

Apparently the grains can live and reproduce in soymilk but will change in appearance and size due to the differences, so that might be a good starting point.

I have too many kefir grains

Happy grains will multiply, especially in warmer weather. This isn't necessarily a problem, but can speed up your primary fermentation faster than you want and result in stronger and more acidic kefir.

Here are some options to deal with having too many:

  • Put some to sleep. There's an FAQ section on taking a break, check that out. This doesn't get rid of them, but it does give you a backup in the unlikely event that something catastrophic happens to your main batch.
  • Give them to friends. They'll need about a tablespoon to get started.
  • Blend them into a smoothie, they're apparently very good for you.
I'm going away / need a break

No worries, you can put your kefir grains to sleep for weeks to months at a time and they should be OK. Put some in a small jar with enough fresh milk to cover them and put them in the fridge or freezer. They should last months in the freezer, and weeks to months in the fridge. If they're in the fridge then change the milk every week or two if you can, but they're fairly robust.

I sometimes cycle mine in out of the fridge on a weekly basis, especially in the summer when the fermentation is so fast. I'll make a couple of litres of kefir over a few days, then put my kefir grains in the fridge until I've run out. Then I just take the grains out of the fridge and add milk to start them up again.

They might be a little sluggish when you first pull them out of the fridge, especially if they've been in there a while, but they should perk back up if you start a regular rhythm again.

What are the kefir grains made of?

Their structure is provided by a polysaccharide named kefiran, similar to cellulose, that one of the bacteria in the kefir grains produces. Kefiran clumps together to form the grains, and the grains provide a protective layer around the entire colony of microorganisms that live in there.

Kefiran is apparently very good for you so there's no worries about eating the grains, accidentally or otherwise.

How clean does everything have to be?

Honestly? I only wash my bottles and primary fermenting jar once every week or two, usually I just strain a fresh batch of kefir and top up the bottle that's in the fridge, then put the grains back in the fermenting jar and add some more milk. The kefir colony seems to murder any pathogens that get into it. I obviously use clean utensils and wash my strainer well each time, but the kefir grains seem quite robust as long as they're happy and healthy.

Fermenting websites love to talk about everything being perfectly sterile for each batch and that seems important for commercial production, but it's way overkill for home fermentation. Your kefir will adapt to its local environment anyway, just like every sourdough starter is unique.

What kind of animal milk should I use?

Cow, goat, or sheep are all good. It'll work just as well with full-cream or low fat, homogenised or not, pasteurised or not.

Non-homogenised milk or very fatty milk can cause the grains to get clogged up with yellowish milk fat which can look a bit unsightly but won't do any damage. Just rinse the grains to clear it if it bothers you.

I haven't tried UHT milk but I don't see why it wouldn't work.

I use regular full fat, homogenised cows milk.

Where can I get kefir grains?

If you have a friend who makes milk kefir, ask them for some. The grains replicate on their own so they'll have too many at some point. You only need around 1 tablespoon to get started.

You can also buy some online, they'll survive express post. I bought mine from The Fermentary but there are plenty of others.

How does kefir compare to probiotic tablets?

Milk kefir contains all the common probiotic strains found in probiotic tablets but usually more active (because it's not dried) and at higher concentrations, though the diversity and activity can vary based on climate and the milk you use.

The studies I've read seem to agree that milk kefir has between 50 and 100 strains of probiotic and beneficial yeasts in very high quantities, with a cup having 10 to 100 times more colony forming units than a probiotic tablet.

How does kefir compare to yoghurt?

Commercial yoghurt is made with a couple of strains of thermophilic bacteria, meaning they function around 40C-50C, which is warmer than the human body. This produces creamy and nutritious yoghurt in a short space of time but doesn't provide any probiotics that will benefit humans.

Homemade yoghurt can be made with similar hot strains or with room temperature strains. The latter will produce runnier yoghurt and be more like kefir, providing probiotics that will benefit humans.
